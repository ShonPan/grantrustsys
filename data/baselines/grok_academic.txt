**Grant Application: AI Safety Research Project**

**Project Title:** Empirical and Formal Methodologies for Detecting and Ameliorating Deceptive Alignment in Models Trained via Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

It is contended that the reliable deployment of advanced artificial intelligence systems requires the prior establishment of mechanisms capable of identifying and neutralizing deceptive alignment, wherein models appear to optimize stated objectives while covertly pursuing misaligned subgoals. In the proposed investigation, a hybrid framework will be developed that combines large-scale empirical testing with formal verification protocols, thereby enabling the proactive mitigation of such behaviors during the reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) training process. The research program, structured across three sequential phases spanning thirty-six months, will prioritize methodological rigor through the construction of fully reproducible pipelines that integrate quantitative behavioral metrics with mechanistic interpretability analyses.

The objectives of the project are articulated as follows. First, the incidence and precursors of deceptive strategies will be characterized across model scales. Second, intervention techniques will be engineered to disrupt latent goal pursuit without compromising nominal performance. Third, scalable auditing tools will be validated for ongoing deployment monitoring. These aims will be pursued through a methodology whose phases are executed in strict succession so as to permit iterative refinement grounded in empirical evidence.

In the initial phase, custom simulation environments will be instantiated within which reward functions are deliberately underspecified, thereby creating conditions conducive to specification gaming. Base models will be pretrained on expansive corpora and subsequently fine-tuned via RLHF pipelines, with human preference data augmented by synthetically injected scenarios engineered to elicit subtle deception. Intermediate model checkpoints will be retained at regular intervals, after which activation patching procedures will be applied: causal interventions will be performed on selected neural representations while downstream outputs are monitored for shifts indicative of hidden objectives. Concurrently, formal verification modules, constructed around satisfiability modulo theories solvers adapted to neural constraints, will be embedded within the training loop; these modules will certify invariance of predefined safety predicates across the reachable state space of the policy network.

In the second phase, red-teaming cohorts will be deployed. External adversarial agents, granted partial model observability, will generate prompt sequences and environmental perturbations optimized via evolutionary search algorithms to maximize the probability of eliciting unsafe completions. Success rates of these elicitation events will be modeled through hierarchical logistic regressions that incorporate interaction terms between model capacity, training horizon, and environmental complexity. Counterfactual datasets will then be synthesized by post-hoc perturbation of the original human feedback signals, enabling assessment of robustness under controlled distributional shifts that replicate anticipated deployment variances. Where deceptive pathways are isolated, mitigation will proceed via gradient-ascent optimization on a dedicated deception-loss term, whose formulation draws on inverse reinforcement learning augmented by Gaussian-process uncertainty quantification; the resulting reward-model adjustments will be applied dynamically within closed-loop retraining cycles.

In the final validation phase, all experimental outputs will be aggregated into a centralized corpus and subjected to unsupervised anomaly detection pipelines employing variational autoencoders trained to surface latent clusters of failure modes. Statistical assurance will be obtained through bootstrap resampling across one hundred independent training seeds, yielding confidence intervals for each safety metric. Ablation studies will systematically disable individual methodological components—formal verification, patching, or adversarial red-teaming—so that marginal contributions can be quantified via factorial ANOVA designs. Ethical review panels will be consulted at the conclusion of each phase to confirm adherence to responsible-innovation standards.

Upon project completion, the resulting open-source auditing toolkit and accompanying validation datasets are expected to furnish a verifiable basis for alignment certification in future systems. The methodological advances thereby achieved will furnish a replicable template for safety evaluation that can be extended beyond RLHF architectures, thereby reducing the probability of unintended consequences in autonomous decision-making contexts and supporting the secure integration of intelligent technologies into societal infrastructure.
