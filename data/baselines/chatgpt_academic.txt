This proposal outlines a research program aimed at advancing methodological foundations for AI safety through the systematic development and evaluation of scalable oversight and alignment techniques. The central objective is to investigate how robust control mechanisms can be designed for increasingly autonomous systems under conditions of uncertainty, limited interpretability, and distributional shift.

The project will be structured around three interrelated workstreams. First, a formal framework for specifying safety constraints in high-dimensional decision-making systems will be developed. Emphasis will be placed on the construction of constraint representations that remain stable under model generalization. Techniques from probabilistic modeling and formal verification will be adapted to enable the encoding of safety-relevant properties as soft and hard constraints within learning architectures. It is anticipated that this framework will allow for systematic comparison between constraint formulations in terms of expressivity, tractability, and empirical reliability.

Second, a suite of scalable oversight mechanisms will be designed and empirically evaluated. In particular, methods for decomposing complex tasks into verifiable subcomponents will be explored, such that oversight signals can be efficiently generated even when end-to-end evaluation is infeasible. Synthetic environments will be constructed in which ground-truth alignment properties are known, allowing for controlled experimentation. Within these environments, hierarchical evaluation protocols will be implemented, and their sensitivity to adversarial optimization will be rigorously tested. Metrics will be defined to quantify oversight fidelity, including error propagation rates and robustness to strategic manipulation by learned policies.

Third, interpretability tools will be integrated into the training and evaluation pipeline to enable fine-grained analysis of internal model representations. Mechanistic interpretability techniques will be extended to identify latent features associated with unsafe behavior patterns. Experimental procedures will be designed in which interventions at the representation level are performed, and subsequent behavioral changes are measured. This will allow causal relationships between internal structures and external actions to be inferred, thereby informing the design of more transparent and controllable systems.

Across all workstreams, a consistent methodological emphasis will be placed on reproducibility and stress-testing under adversarial conditions. Models will be evaluated not only on standard performance metrics but also on their behavior under distributional perturbations and constraint violations. Ablation studies will be conducted to isolate the contribution of individual components, and statistical methods will be employed to assess the significance and generalizability of observed effects.

The expected outcome of this research is a set of rigorously validated methodologies for improving the safety and reliability of advanced AI systems. By prioritizing formalization, empirical validation, and interpretability, the project seeks to contribute foundational tools that can be adapted across a wide range of application domains. Ultimately, it is intended that these contributions will support the development of AI systems whose behavior remains aligned with specified objectives even in complex and uncertain environments.
