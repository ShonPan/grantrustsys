**1. Project Summary**

It is proposed that a novel methodological framework be developed for the formal verification of intervention techniques applied within the latent representational spaces of large-scale generative artificial intelligence systems. While current approaches to AI safety increasingly emphasize the promise of "steering" or "circuit-breaking" via inference-time interventions, a critical gap is identified in the absence of rigorous, formally verifiable guarantees concerning the stability, specificity, and generalizability of such modifications. This project seeks to address this gap through the construction of a hybrid analytical architecture, wherein techniques drawn from mechanistic interpretability are integrated with tools from formal methods and distributional robustness theory. The anticipated outcome is a transferable verification protocol capable of establishing probabilistic safety certificates for model behaviors post-intervention, thereby providing a foundational layer of assurance absent from existing empirical methodologies.

**2. Background and Rationale**

Contemporary safety research has demonstrated that undesired behaviors in large language and multimodal models—such as the generation of hazardous content or the circumvention of safety classifiers—can be mitigated through targeted modifications to their internal activations. However, the dominant paradigm remains fundamentally empirical: interventions are validated through the application of static hold-out test sets. A fundamental limitation of this approach is recognized, as it fails to account for adversarial distributional shifts, compositional generalization failures, or the emergence of unintended collateral capabilities. Consequently, a demonstrable need exists for a methodology that can shift the evidentiary basis for safety interventions from one of post-hoc statistical observation to one of *a priori* structural guarantee.

**3. Methodology and Technical Approach**

The research will be executed across three integrated work packages, each designed to address a distinct facet of the verification problem.

In the first work package, a formal representation of the target model's latent space will be constructed. It is intended that this representation move beyond simplistic linear approximations; instead, a framework of sparse autoencoders will be utilized to decompose activations into a high-dimensional dictionary of interpretable, monosemantic features. These features will serve as the atomic units upon which safety properties are defined. The selection of these features will be conducted iteratively, with validation performed through controlled causal tracing experiments to ensure functional fidelity.

The second work package concerns the development of a verification engine. A dual-layer methodology is to be employed. First, a symbolic abstraction layer will be defined, wherein the continuous geometry of the latent feature space is mapped onto a discrete transition system amenable to model checking. Within this layer, safety specifications—such as the guarantee that a particular feature vector cannot be activated above a defined threshold following a steering intervention—will be encoded in Linear Temporal Logic (LTL). Second, a probabilistic robustness layer will be instantiated using tools from randomized smoothing and scenario optimization. This layer is intended to provide high-confidence bounds on the intervention's stability under bounded adversarial perturbations to the input, effectively quantifying the radius of assured safety.

The third work package involves the empirical instantiation and evaluation of the integrated framework. A suite of open-source generative models will be selected as test subjects. For each, a specific safety-critical behavior (e.g., refusal to comply with instructions for malicious code generation) will be isolated. A steering intervention will be learned to suppress this behavior via a differentiable objective function. Crucially, the verification protocol developed in the preceding packages will then be applied *prior* to deployment. The primary metric for success will be the absence of false negatives within the verified safety certificate—that is, the protocol's demonstrated capacity to identify, through formal analysis, counterexamples or failure modes not captured by conventional empirical test suites. Comparative analysis against standard adversarial attack frameworks will be conducted to establish the relative rigor of the proposed approach.

**4. Evaluation and Dissemination**

Success will be measured by the ability to generate provable certificates for non-trivial safety properties in models of scale. All code artifacts, formal specifications, and verification results will be released under an open-science framework. Findings are to be disseminated in venues specializing in machine learning, formal methods, and AI safety, with the objective of establishing a new cross-disciplinary standard for model behavior verification.

**5. Broader Impact and Timelines**

The significance of this work lies in its potential to establish a methodological precedent for safety certification, moving the field toward standards of assurance commensurate with those in other safety-critical engineering disciplines. A 24-month timeline is projected, with dedicated milestones for framework design, prototype implementation, and rigorous adversarial validation.
