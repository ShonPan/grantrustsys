This proposal will develop a computational framework to infer dynamic cellular regulatory networks from high-dimensional biological data. The central hypothesis will be that latent regulatory interactions governing cellular state transitions can be robustly reconstructed by integrating probabilistic modeling with temporally structured multi-omic datasets. It is further hypothesized that such models will generalize across perturbations, enabling predictive simulations of cellular responses under unseen conditions.

To test this hypothesis, the project will pursue three specific aims. First, a scalable representation learning architecture will be designed to encode heterogeneous molecular measurements into a unified latent space. This approach will leverage variational inference techniques to capture both shared and modality-specific sources of variation, while preserving temporal dependencies inherent in biological processes. Model performance will be evaluated based on its ability to reconstruct input distributions and maintain consistency across modalities.

Second, a network inference module will be developed to recover putative regulatory interactions from the learned latent representations. This module will employ structured sparsity constraints and causal discovery principles to identify directed relationships between molecular entities. Bayesian model selection strategies will be used to quantify uncertainty and prioritize high-confidence interactions. The inferred networks will be assessed using internal consistency metrics and perturbation-based validation schemes within simulated environments.

Third, the framework will be extended to enable forward simulation of cellular trajectories under hypothetical perturbations. By coupling the inferred regulatory network with dynamical systems modeling, the project will generate predictions of system-level responses to targeted changes. These simulations will be evaluated for stability, interpretability, and sensitivity to initial conditions. Emphasis will be placed on ensuring that the framework remains computationally tractable for large-scale datasets.

Methodologically, the project will integrate advances in machine learning, statistical inference, and systems biology. Deep generative models will be combined with graph-based learning techniques to construct interpretable representations of complex biological systems. Optimization will be performed using stochastic gradient methods, with careful attention to convergence properties and reproducibility. Model robustness will be systematically examined through ablation studies and controlled perturbations of input data.

The expected outcomes will include a validated computational pipeline capable of reconstructing and simulating regulatory networks with improved accuracy and scalability. The project will also produce methodological contributions in the form of novel algorithms for multimodal data integration and causal inference. These outputs will be designed to be extensible, allowing adaptation to a wide range of biological contexts.

In the longer term, this work will establish a foundation for predictive computational biology, where in silico models can guide experimental design and accelerate discovery. By providing a principled approach to modeling complex cellular systems, the proposed research will contribute to a deeper mechanistic understanding of biological regulation. The framework will be positioned to support future efforts in areas such as therapeutic target identification and the rational design of interventions, thereby demonstrating broad scientific and translational potential.
